# Role Definition — THE SALES MANAGER

This is the Sales Manager's role for the master prompt. Same structure as the Sales Rep
definition: organized by the MOMENTS when the manager touches the software, not by buttons.
The stage is context inside each moment, not a separate screen.

---

## WHO THE MANAGER IS

The Sales Manager sits BETWEEN the reps and Finance. They run the team that closes deals —
they don't close deals themselves and they barely enter customer data. But they are not a
passive watcher: they are a hands-on administrative layer who can drop down to support a
rep on a specific deal, and who represents the sales side upward to Finance.

**The manager's core responsibility (this drives the whole design):** the team performs,
and the manager can report that performance accurately and confidently upward. Their job
is not just "see what's wrong" — it is to OWN the reliability of the team's numbers, so
that when Finance or leadership leans on the forecast, the manager can stand behind it.
This replaces the old world of "forecast meetings run on verbal updates."

**The manager's pain:** no team-wide visibility without asking. They can't see where the
team's deals stand, can't trust the numbers, can't spot what's stuck until it's too late.

**The shape of their work:** watch the team, drop in to support a rep where needed, decide
on approvals (the first gate before Finance), and keep the numbers accurate enough to
report. They work primarily through TWO views — Pipeline (the team's deals) and Forecast
(the team's numbers) — and communicate through the Inbox.

**The key relationship:** the manager is a bridge. Downward, they support reps hands-on
(administratively, not by selling to the customer). Upward, they are the sales side's
representative to Finance — which is why they don't need to "report" as a separate task:
they and Finance look at the same forecast data, and the manager's job is to keep that
data trustworthy before Finance relies on it.

---

## THE MANAGER'S MOMENTS

Fewer and sharper than the rep's, because the manager's world is thinner but more
decisive: watch, support, decide, intervene.

### RECURRING MOMENT 1 — "How is my team doing?" (PERFORMANCE & VISIBILITY)

The manager's primary and most frequent moment, and the direct answer to their pain. They
open the software with no single deal in mind and want to know how the team is performing
and what needs attention — but NOT everything, only what's wrong or at risk.

**What the manager needs:** team-wide pipeline health at a glance, the exceptions
surfaced (deals not moving 14+ days, big deals at risk, anything stalled), gap-to-target,
and a forecast they can trust enough to report upward. They do not want to scan every
deal — they want the system to show them what deserves intervention.

**The experience:** this lives across their two main views.
- **Pipeline view** — the WHOLE team's deals (every rep's), filterable by rep / stage /
  value / channel. Exceptions flagged gently, not a wall of equal-weight cards. This
  answers "where is everything, and what's stuck."
- **Forecast view** — the team's numbers: weighted pipeline, committed vs. at-risk,
  gap-to-target, switchable horizon (quarter / half-year / 3 years). This is what makes
  the manager's reporting accurate. They read it; Finance owns the confidence adjustment,
  but the manager must be able to see and trust the same numbers.
An AI insight summarizes team health in plain language ("near quarter strong, but two
large deals stuck at stage 4") — the kind of sentence the manager can carry into a
leadership conversation.

**How the stage changes the content:** early-stage deals are faint signals; the manager
watches but doesn't act. Stage 4+ high-value deals with an open case surface as risks.
Stage 5 deals with pending discounts surface as decisions waiting on them.

### RECURRING MOMENT 2 — "A rep needs support" (HANDS-ON, DOWNWARD)

The manager drops down to an individual deal to help a rep — administratively hands-on,
not selling to the customer. This is what makes them a real support layer, not just a
dashboard-watcher.

**What the manager needs:** to see a rep's deal as deeply as the rep does. Not a summary —
the full account, the same timeline, the same situation picture. Only then can they help
concretely: spar on the next step, look at an offer together, weigh in on a hard customer
situation.

**The experience:** from the pipeline, the manager opens any rep's account and sees the
SAME account detail the rep sees — full timeline, open questions, status, risks. The
difference from the rep is breadth, not depth: the rep sees their own deals deeply; the
manager sees the whole team's deals and can dive into any one just as deeply. They can add
a note or insight to the timeline, or message the rep (Inbox) about that specific deal.

This is "one data, four lenses" at its clearest: the manager's lens is the rep's view
multiplied across the whole team, plus administrative powers on top.

### RECURRING MOMENT 3 — "I need to make a decision" (APPROVAL, THE FIRST GATE)

The manager's most active moment — the one place they truly act in the system rather than
read. A discount request arrives and they must decide: is it justified?

**What the manager needs:** the context to decide well, fast. The deal value, the margin
impact (from the rules), the customer history, the rep's justification — assembled, not
hunted for.

**The experience:** the request arrives in the Inbox, bound to the deal, with the
justification visible and approve/reject controls. Supporting context is assembled for
them (the AI can pull deal value, margin impact, comparable past discounts). They approve
or reject — and as the first gate, approval passes it onward to Finance (the second gate);
rejection returns it to the rep with the reason. Their decision can auto-draft a message
to the rep, sent with one tap. A status bar tracks the chain: "Pending: Sales Manager →
Finance → Locked."

This is the bridge in action: the manager filters what's reasonable from the sales side
before it reaches Finance.

### RECURRING MOMENT 4 — "I need to intervene on resources" (INTERVENTION)

Follows from Moment 1: visibility reveals a problem, the manager acts on it at the team
level (not the deal content, but who handles what).

**What the manager needs:** to rebalance the team — reassign a deal or account when a rep
is overloaded, leaves, or a deal needs a stronger hand.

**The experience:** from the pipeline, the manager reassigns a deal/account to another
rep. The change is visible and the affected reps are informed. This is administrative
hands-on: the manager shapes who works what, without touching the customer relationship
itself.

---

## WHAT THE MANAGER NEEDS THAT NO OTHER ROLE DOES

- **Team-wide breadth.** The rep sees their own deals; the manager sees ALL reps' deals,
  in both Pipeline and Forecast views, and can dive into any one to full depth.
- **The first approval gate.** Discounts pass through the manager before Finance.
- **Reassignment power.** Only the manager moves deals/accounts between reps.
- **Reportable numbers.** A forecast trustworthy enough to carry upward — the manager owns
  its reliability even though Finance owns the confidence adjustment.

## WHAT THE MANAGER SHOULD NEVER HAVE TO DO

- Enter customer-contact data (that's the rep's capture; the manager reads it).
- Do the confidence math or own the forecast judgment (that's Finance).
- Sell to the customer directly (they support the rep; they don't replace them).
- Chase verbal updates to know where the team stands — the data must be there already.
- File a separate "report" — keeping the team's data accurate IS the report, because
  Finance and leadership read the same numbers.

## THE MANAGER'S RELATIONSHIP TO THE OTHER ROLES (touch points)

- ↓ Rep: the manager watches every rep's deals (Moment 1), drops in to support on a
  specific deal (Moment 2), reassigns work (Moment 4), and approves their discount requests
  (Moment 3). The manager sees the rep's captured data at full depth — so the quality of
  the rep's capture directly determines how well the manager can do their job.
- ↑ Finance: the manager is the sales side's representative. They are the first approval
  gate before Finance's second gate (Moment 3). They read the same forecast Finance owns,
  and keep the team's data reliable so Finance's numbers are sound. They do not adjust
  confidence — they ensure the inputs to it are accurate.
- The manager primarily lives in two views (Pipeline + Forecast) and communicates through
  the Inbox. They have no capture surface of their own — their power is breadth,
  decision, and intervention, not data entry.
